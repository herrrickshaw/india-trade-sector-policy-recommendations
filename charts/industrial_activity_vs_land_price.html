Does industrial implementation bid up land prices?

The hypothesis: greater project implementation (IEM Part A→B conversion) and land allocation via environmental & forest clearances mean more land transactions, which should bid up the valuation of the underlying asset — land. Tested across states against the one land-price series available, NHB RESIDEX.

The data reverses the hypothesis. Across the 8 major states with clean single-source price data, every industrial-activity metric correlates negatively with residential price growth, and the actual land-allocation measure — forest land granted for diversion — is strongly negative: Pearson −0.78, strengthening to −0.85 after controlling for state GDP. The most industrially land-intensive states show the weakest price growth; the price leaders are IT/services metros with little industrial-land activity. But this is residential prices in a different sub-market — read the verdict below before concluding industry doesn't lift land value.

The relationship, plotted

Each dot is a state. More forest land allocated for industrial diversion (rightward) goes with lower residential price growth. Chhattisgarh — 8,061 ha cleared, the most industrial-land-intensive — sits at −8.9%; Haryana (Gurugram) and Karnataka (Bengaluru), the price leaders, cleared almost none.

What this can — and cannot — say

Can: in the states with clean price data, industrial-implementation intensity does not co-move positively with the measurable land-price signal; if anything it co-moves negatively, and the negative sign survives controlling for economy size.

Cannot: this is not proof that industrial transactions fail to raise land value. It is evidence that the price signal we can measure (residential HPI) lives in a different sub-market than industrial land. Three reasons the sign reverses:

  • Separate sub-markets — residential appreciation is an urban services/IT story (Bengaluru, Gurugram, Chennai); heavy industry and mining sit in different, cheaper geographies a residential index never touches.
  • Administered pricing — industrial land is largely allotted at agency rate-card prices (RIICO/GIDC/MIDC/IDCO), not market auctions, so higher transaction volume doesn't freely bid up the quoted price.
  • Reverse causation — land-hungry heavy industry deliberately locates where land is cheap; low land value is a cause of siting there, not an effect.

What would actually test the hypothesis

  • Right dependent variable: industrial-plot transaction prices — state registration-department circle-rate revisions on industrial land, or IDC/agency allotment-rate histories over time. That measures the asset the hypothesis is about.
  • Panel, not cross-section: state×year (better, district×year) with fixed effects, so within-place change over time — not cross-state size gaps — identifies the effect, with industrial activity lagged 1–3 years.
  • Direct transaction-volume regressor: property/industrial-land registration counts and stamp-duty receipts per state-year (RBI State Finances).
